POWERFUL WOMEN

Priscilla Chan

Priscilla Chan is an American Pediatrician and Philanthropist, she graduated from Harvard University with a Bachelors Degree in Biology. She later taught science at the private Harker School for a year. Priscilla went back to school and received her medical degree from the University of California, San Francisco (UCSF). Even though her husband is Mark Zuckerberg, Priscilla is also making her own moves.

Katherine Johnson

Katherine Johnson is an Amercian mathematician, she graduated from West Virginia State College with a Bachelors Degree in Mathematics and French. She then was hired by NACA/ NASA in 1953. Katherine’s mathematical calculations of orbital mechanics at NASA had a great impact on the success of the first U.S. space flights.

Stacey Cunningham

Stacey Cunningham as child was always one of the few females that were interested in science and mathematics in school. She graduated from Lehigh University with a Bachelors Degree in Industrial Engineering. Now Stacey is a banker and the first female president of the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE).

Mary Jackson

Mary Jackson was an African American mathematician and aerospace engineer, she graduated from Hampton Institute with a Bachelor of Science Degree in Mathematics and Physical Science. She was first hired by NASA in 1951 and did a lot of computing work, but after taking advanced engineering classes Mary became NASA’s first female African American engineer in 1958.

Liu Qing

Liu Qing is a Chinese business executive, she graduated from Peking University with a Bachelors Degree in Computer Science, a Masters Degree in Computer Science from Harvard University, and then an honorary Doctor of Commercial Science from New York University. Liu is now the President of Didi Chuxing, China’s largest mobile transportation platform.

Dorothy Vaughan

Dorothy Vaughan was an African American mathematician and human computer, she graduated from Wilberforce with a Bachelors Degree in Mathematics. She was the first African American woman to supervise a group of staff in the research center. Dorothy taught herself and a group of women how to read a certain programming language which made her the head of the programming section.

Jacinda Ardern

Jacinda Arden is a New Zealand Politician, she graduated from the University of Waikato with a Bachelors of Communication Studies in politics and public relations. Jacinda is serving as the 40th Prime Minister since October 2017 and also serving as the Leader of the Labor Party since August 2017.

OPPORTUNITY PAGE

Below are opportunities for young women that are interested in different STEM fields or young women that are just trying to find out what careers are for them.

  • Girls Who Code Summer Immersion Program
    • Girls Who Code is an international nonprofit working to close the gender gap in technology. Their Summer Immersion Program began in 2012 in New York City, offering rising 11th and 12th grade girls 300+ hours of immersive instruction in web development and design, robotics, and mobile development with mentorship and exposure to top female engineers and entrepreneurs.

  • Boston Leadership Institute (Boston, MA)
    • The Boston Leadership Institute offers need-based, full scholarships to a limited number of highly qualified teenagers who wish to participate in select three-week STEM research programs.

  • Nepris
    • Nepris is a free online tool that virtually connects students to educators in order to bring real world relevance and career exposure to all students. Nepris also provides a skills-based volunteering platform for organizations to extend education outreach, and build their brand among the future workforce.

  • Invent it. Build it. society of women engineers
    • All attendees will spend the morning at the STEM EXPO, where they can learn about local engineering clubs, engineering camps, competitions, after-school programs, scholarships and more. In the afternoon, Invent it. Build it. is divided into three parts: the SWE Middle School Girls Program, the Parent/Educator Program (PEP) and the High School Girls Program. Each program is designed to engage the audience in STEM activities and provide them with resources that will help them, their students or their children pursue a path in engineering and technology.

  • Girlstart
    • Girls currently in 4th-5th & 6th-8th grades will discover the fun side of STEM careers at this unique annual conference just for them. The workshop is packed with three hands-on workshops led by women in science, technology, engineering, and math fields.

Addressing those is a key to moving forward—but it’s only part of the picture. To increase gender diversity in STEM, there’s a latent, untapped opportunity staring us in the face: girls’ strong desire to change the world. We just need to help girls connect the dots between changing the world and STEM.
